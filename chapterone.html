Chapter 1

You're fucking tired. And not that "stayed up until 3am closing down the bar" tired: waking up mid-afternoon in a haze, desperate for carbs, distracted by sex with the brunette next to you. Remembering that, actually, you didn't fall asleep until 6am. When you look at your phone later, you'll have a bunch of texts recapping the highlights of the night before. Dozens of photos, and, theoretically, memories you'll have forever, even though you haven't had your first meal of the day yet, and you're already forgetting.

Not that tired.

It's 4pm, and there's a little girl next to you screaming her head off. It's your problem. She's yours. It's your living room she's drowning in apple sauce; her coated fingers launching the plastic jar directly at a photo of your mother. The small hanging frame is hit dead center and drops with a crash to the floor. You can see a large jagged crack down the front of the glass.

Memories flash of first time you had dinner with your husband's family. His mother talked for hours about how difficult he used to be, so impossible when he was a child, and then pop! Suddenly he turned into the best, most understanding, uber-considerate son ever— the one who called his mother once a week, the one who helped his father with repairs around his childhood home. You'd put your hand on top of his and smiled at his embarrassment, disbelieving laughter bubbling out of your chest at how this child had somehow turned into your perfect boyfriend.

Now, you're not fucking laughing.

Phoebe, your amber-eyed, button-nosed girl with ringlets like her father, screamed when she entered the world and hasn't stopped since. When she was six months old, everyone told you that it would get better. At two, they laughed, "they call it terrible two's for a reason!" Now, at four, they said nothing, because no one wanted to be around them. Those carefully cultivated new-mom friendships gone, your daughter deemed some kind of bad influence on their good, silent children.

You get up slowly from the play mat on the living room floor. This tantrum's focus, the silver bracelet you didn't allow to be dipped in apple sauce and swallowed whole, twinkles against your wrist. The paper towel is just a few feet away on the kitchen island. That doesn't stop you from banging your hip again on the corner of the granite countertop that, despite five years of being in this place, your body seems to hone in on. Every. Time. At this point, you barely register the pain from the semi-permanent bruise above your hip—but today, you're knocked off balance and your wine glass drops.

'Big noise.' That's what you and your husband used to say to your daughter when trains passed by, smoothies were blended, thunder boomed. You'd cover your ears in mock protection and your little girl would follow, eyes big and mouth a tiny o in wonder at all the sounds that could happen in the world.

Red spreads across the ash wood floor. Your daughter screams in a new pitch, as if to keep in key with this other, competing noise. You get around the island and grab the paper towel, dropping to your knees to soak up the wine and pick up the bits of glass, once of which slices through your finger. Blood drips, and you sigh so deeply you feel it in your toes. It's moments like this you miss your husband.

You finish grabbing bits of glass and mopping up wine, going over the area with a vacuum three times because your daughter refuses to wear shoes. Afterwards, you walk to the couch on the other side of the room and sit at the end of the L that keeps the play mat in sight. Lean back, let the worn in leather catch you, let yourself be catch-able. The fan above limps along, one of those remote-powered ones with modes and levels.

You grab the remote off the end table and jack it up to high. The fan squeaks with speed. Your house is an ongoing project. Not the dream house you were promised when your husband was down on one knee, but workable. Workable is underrated. The fan turns into a blur and you stare at it, turning your wedding ring around on your finger. It still sparkles like the day you got it, somehow. You should take it off. You don't.

You remember the last time you saw your husband. The way he stumbled, eyes dark and empty. Spewing words at you, at your daughter. A year later you still feel shame at ignoring all the signs. Guess that difficult little boy didn't disappear, after all.

But the funny thing is, you like your life now. Things are no longer new and exciting. You're no longer dazzled by thoughts of the future, all those possibilities life has to offer, but there's no more pretending. How much of your life before this was pretending? Posturing for a guy, a job, hell, strangers on the street. Trying to display the right signs. Fuck it. At least things were real now.

Even your daughter, scream as she might, was perfect. Her curls, her ever-moving hands, her little bow mouth. Those eyes. You tried your best at balancing the mother you wanted to be—mainly, what your mother wasn't—with the logistics of being an adult. You never wanted to be the voice telling her to be quiet, tamed, and managed for other people's comfort, to not be alarmed by the world. Why shouldn't she be? Bad things happen every day. People get sick, lose their jobs, their homes. Wildfires and hurricanes ravage. Poverty persists. Meanwhile, in your home, laundry piled, and dishes towered, and you tried to feed your girl things that in 25 years they wouldn't say kills people. So yeah, your daughter screamed, but honestly, after you ate, breathed, were for two for nine months, maybe she was just repaying the favor, screaming for the both of you. Because the truth was, if you wanted anything for her, it was to be strong. A warrior. To not bend and contort herself. To feel and withstand. And when it's all too much, when it's raging inside her, with no where else to go? You wanted your daughter to just fucking scream.

Your daughter isn't screaming.

You jerk upright. The bright orange mat where your daughter was sitting is empty. Crayons discarded. Picture unfinished. Your body fills with a bitter ice you can taste.

You call out your daughter's name. Again. And again. Your eyes whip around the room, search the dark crevices for movement.

Nothing.

You'd only looked away for a second.

You move with a speed you didn't know was available to you before having children. People don't tell you that about becoming a parent, a whole other level to your body becomes available. Cars become liftable. Rooms are crossable in seconds.

Resentment boils for every piece of furniture, every wall preventing you from finding her. You're pushing things around, going up and down the hallway—thank god there's only one floor—raising your voice but trying to keep it within a range that's acceptable, that the neighbors would hear and roll their eyes at, if you still had neighbors. Those people being loud again.

You're in her bedroom, ripping open the closet dotted with sparkly stickers, lifting the tiny table and chairs that hosted afternoon tea time, even though you can clearly see she's not there. Not in the bathroom, across her bedroom door, sitting in the bathtub or under the sink. Not in your old office, hiding under the desk your husband bought you back when you worked and had a husband. The door to the attic in the ceiling of the hallway is firmly shut.

You're very, very scared, and that fear has an intelligence all its own: How far could she have gone? How long had you not been paying attention? What's the matter with you?

where is she where is she where is she

Turning around and around in the hallway, each path feeling like the wrong one, a waste of time, you're losing time. You're about to yell her name again, with a breath that scrapes you raw to your core.

And then you hear it.

A giggle.

You run down the hall, eyes raking over the open living room and kitchen. There—she's there, visible through the window in the kitchen to your right, above the stove. She sits on the driveway, looking up at the big blue sky, eyeing the edges of the world.

You don't remember pushing yourself through the living room, out of the front door, around the side of the house to the driveway. There's no car in the driveway, no people walking the sidewalk beside you two. Of course not.

The moments you lose sight of her put your heart in a vise and you push harder, until you round the corner and you're wrapping your arms around her, bringing her close into your chest, and she's fine, but you're trying not to cry.

You can feel words rising up, anger that's really fear, but for now, you're okay, Lila's okay.

Everything is good.

Everything is okay.

Everything is safe.
